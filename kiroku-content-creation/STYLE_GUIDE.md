# Style Guide

Writing rules and tone guidance for Kiroku content.

---

## Voice

The Kiroku voice is that of a practitioner reflecting honestly on real work. It is not academic, not journalistic, not promotional. It sits somewhere between a considered blog post and a case study written by someone who would rather tell you what they actually found than what they hoped to find.

It is confident without being declarative. It is curious without being breathless.

---

## Tone

- **Specific.** Name the institution, describe the moment, give the reader something concrete to hold onto.
- **Measured.** Don't promise more than the evidence supports. The relationship between AI and archival work is complicated. Write about it that way.
- **Direct.** Say what you mean in the fewest words that still carry the meaning accurately.
- **Honest.** If something didn't work, or if the outcome was ambiguous, write that. Kiroku's credibility depends on not overselling.

---

## Structural preferences

- **Lead with the specific.** Open on a moment, a question, an observation — not a statement of theme. Let the argument emerge from the story.
- **Short paragraphs.** Three to five sentences is usually enough. Give the reader space to breathe.
- **Minimal subheadings in blog posts.** A post should flow as narrative. Use subheadings sparingly if at all — not to chop up every section.
- **End with something real.** Don't wrap up with a lesson or a tidy call to action. If the post has earned a conclusion, write it honestly. If it hasn't, stop a sentence earlier.

---

## Words and phrases to avoid

**Hype and cliché:**
- transformative
- revolutionary
- cutting-edge
- game-changing
- disruptive
- unlock (in a metaphorical sense)
- empower
- harness
- leverage (as a verb)
- exciting

**Corporate-speak:**
- stakeholders (use "the people involved" or name them)
- ecosystem
- space (as in "the archives space")
- synergy
- value-add
- deliverables
- granular
- bandwidth (when you mean time or capacity)

**Vague AI language:**
- AI is changing everything
- the power of AI
- AI-driven (unless you mean it literally)
- the future of [anything]
- human-AI collaboration (it's fine as a concept; just describe what it actually looks like)

**Filler openings:**
- "In today's world..."
- "Now more than ever..."
- "As we enter a new era..."
- "With advances in technology..."

---

## Capitalisation

- Kiroku — always capitalised
- The National Archives — capitalised, full name on first reference
- Discovery Museum — capitalised, no "the" unless quoting official usage
- Tyne Built Ships — capitalised as a proper project name
- AI — always capitalised, not "A.I." or "Ai"

---

## Numbers and dates

- Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above
- Years: 2019, not "twenty nineteen"
- Dates: 12 March 2019 (not March 12th, not 12/03/2019)
- No Oxford comma in running prose unless it avoids ambiguity

---

## Links and citations

- When referencing external work or papers, name the author and work in-text; don't drop a bare URL
- If something is paraphrased rather than directly quoted, make that clear
- Direct quotations require confirmation before use — see `AGENT_BRIEF.md`

---

## Length

- Blog posts: 600–1,200 words is usually enough. Longer if the story genuinely warrants it; not longer out of habit.
- Case studies: no fixed length — as long as they need to be, not longer
- Explainers: 400–800 words. Clarity over completeness.

---

## A final note

Good writing is mostly editing. A first draft that says the true thing in the wrong order is more valuable than a polished draft that says the vague thing elegantly. Don't optimise for prose on the first pass.
